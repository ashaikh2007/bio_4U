Population Dynamics



Population dynamics is the study of how and why populations change over time. It involves understanding factors like population size, density, growth rates, and interactions between species.


Population Size and Density

  • Population Size: The total number of individuals in a population.
  • Population Density: The number of individuals per unit area or volume.
  • Formula for Population Density:
    Density = Number of Individuals / Area


    • Sampling Methods

  • Transects:
    • Used for linear habitats (e.g., along a river or forest edge).
    • Example: Counting plant species along a line.
  • Quadrats:
    • Used for stationary organisms (e.g., plants, barnacles).
    • Example: Counting the number of dandelions in a 1m² square.
  • Mark-Recapture Method:
    • Used for mobile organisms (e.g., deer, fish).
    • Formula:
      Population Size = (Number Marked × Total Recaptured) / Number Recaptured with Marks
    • Example: Marking deer with tags and recapturing them to estimate population size.


    • Population Density Patterns

  • Clumped: Individuals grouped in patches (e.g., schools of fish).
  • Uniform: Evenly spaced individuals (e.g., territorial animals).
  • Random: No specific pattern (e.g., wind-dispersed seeds).


    • Survivorship and Reproductive Patterns

  • Fecundity: The potential reproductive capacity of an individual.
  • Survivorship: The percentage of individuals surviving to a certain age.
  • Survivorship Curves:
    • Type I: High survival in early life, rapid decline in old age (e.g., humans).
    • Type II: Constant survival rate throughout life (e.g., birds).
    • Type III: High mortality in early life, few survive to old age (e.g., fish).


    • Population Change

  • Formula:
    Change in Population = (Births + Immigration) - (Deaths + Emigration)
  • Growth Rate: The rate at which a population increases or decreases over time.
  • Formula:
    Growth Rate = (Births - Deaths) / Initial Population Size


    • Biotic Potential and Carrying Capacity

  • Biotic Potential: The maximum reproductive capacity of a population under ideal conditions.
  • Influenced by factors like fecundity, lifespan, and age of reproduction.
  • Carrying Capacity: The maximum population size an environment can sustain.
  • Exponential Growth: Rapid growth in ideal conditions (e.g., bacteria).
  • Logistic Growth: Growth slows as the population approaches carrying capacity (e.g., deer).


    • r-Selected vs. K-Selected Organisms

  • r-Selected:
    • High reproductive rate, short lifespan, many offspring (e.g., insects).
  • K-Selected:
    • Low reproductive rate, long lifespan, few offspring (e.g., elephants).


    • Population Regulation

  • Density-Independent Factors: Affect populations regardless of density (e.g., natural disasters).
  • Density-Dependent Factors: Affect populations based on density (e.g., competition, disease).


    • Competition and Resource Partitioning

  • Intraspecific Competition: Competition within the same species (e.g., deer competing for food).
  • Interspecific Competition: Competition between different species (e.g., lions and hyenas).
  • Competitive Exclusion Principle: Two species cannot occupy the same niche indefinitely.
  • Resource Partitioning: Species divide resources to reduce competition (e.g., birds feeding at different heights).


    • Predator-Prey Cycles

  • Predator and prey populations fluctuate in cycles.
  • Example: Lynx and hare populations in the Arctic.


    • Defense Mechanisms

  • Protective Coloration: Camouflage to blend into the environment (e.g., chameleons).
  • Mimicry: Resembling another organism to avoid predation (e.g., harmless snakes mimicking venomous ones).

    Symbiotic Relationships

  • Parasitism: One organism benefits at the expense of the other (e.g., ticks on dogs).
  • Mutualism: Both organisms benefit (e.g., bees and flowers).
  • Commensalism: One organism benefits, the other is unaffected (e.g., barnacles on whales).


    • Human Population Growth

  • Demography: The study of human populations.
  • Factors contributing to growth: Advanced medicine, better shelter, improved food production.
  • Doubling Time: The time it takes for a population to double in size.
  • Ecological Footprint: The impact of human activities on the environment.
  • Examples: Cropland, grazing land, fishing, carbon absorption, building area.
  • Average ecological footprint: 2 hectares per person.
  • Biocapacity: The Earth's capacity to regenerate resources.
  • Current biocapacity exceeds carrying capacity due to overexploitation.


    • Ecological Concepts

  • Biomagnification: The increase in concentration of toxins up the food chain.
  • Deforestation: The clearing of forests for agriculture or development.
  • Sustainability: Meeting current needs without compromising future generations.
  • Bycatch: Unintended capture of non-target species during fishing.
  • Biodiversity: The variety of life in an ecosystem.
  • Overexploitation: Harvesting resources faster than they can regenerate.
  • Invasive Species: Non-native species that disrupt ecosystems.
