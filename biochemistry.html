Biochemistry



What exactly goes on inside our bodies, on a molecular level? Biochemistry answers this, giving valuable insight into the chemicals and chemical processes that keep us going every day


Chemistry in living organisms

All matter is composed of elements (a substance that cannot be broken down further). Out of the 118 elements that exist, 92 serve as the building blocks of matter. Out of those 92, 6 serve as the chemical foundation for life
  • Carbon (underlying structure of biological molecules)
  • Hydrogen (underlying structure of biological molecules)
  • Nitrogen (provide certain properties to biological molecules)
  • Oxygen (provide certain properties to biological molecules)
  • Phosphorus (provide certain properties to biological molecules)
  • Sulfur (provide certain properties to biological molecules)

    Macromolecules

    Macromolecules make up living things. Many macromolecules are polymers(string of connected monomers). The main macromolecules are
  • Carbohydrates
  • Lipids
  • Proteins
  • Nucleic Acids

    • Carbohydrates

  • Contain carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen in a 1:2:1 ratio
  • Polar (dissolve in water)
  • Can range from simple carbs(monosaccharides) to complex carbs(polysaccharides)

    • Monosaccharides Disaccharides Polysaccharides
  • Composed of single carbon based monomer structure
  • C6H12O6(simple sugar)
  • 3 monosaccharides: glucose, fructose, galactose
  • These monosaccharides are isomers, meaning they have the same chemical formula, but different structures
    		•
  • 2 monosaccharides combined
  • The covalent bond between the two monosaccharides is called glycosidic linkage
  • An example of a disaccharide is table sugar, which is fructose + glucose
    		•
  • Multiple monosaccharides linked together by glycosidic linkage
  • Common polysaccharides are starch, glycogen, and cellulose
  • Glycosidic linkage in cellulose is different from starch and glycogen
  • This is because the hydroxyl group on carbon 1 of glucose can exist in the alpha or beta position
  • The alpha position leads to the formation of starch and glycogen, while beta leads to the formation of cellulose
  • Beta form cannot be broken down by our bodies
    		•


    Lipids(fats)

  • Made up of CHO
  • Hydrophobic
  • High number of C-H bonds make it effective for energy storage
  • Energy is stored within hydrocarbons, and as a result is less accessible than carbs
  • Different types of lipids are triglycerides, phospholipids, steroids, and waxes

    • Triglycerides Phospholipids Steroids Waxes
  • Glycerol backbone(hydrophilic) + 3 fatty acids(hydrophobic)
  • Hydroxyl group on glycerol backbone bonds to carbon group on fatty acid through ester bond, resulting in an ester functional group
  • Fatty acid hydrocarbon chain ends with acidic carboxyl group (COOH). Bonds within this result in either an unsaturated or saturated fatty acid
  • Saturated fats: Single bond between carbons. Usually solid at room temperature(eg. butter)
  • Unsaturated fats: Either single double bond(monounsaturated) or multiple double bonds (polyunsaturated). Usually liquid at room temp(eg. oil)
    		•
  • Makes up cell membranes
  • Form lipid bilayer in aqueous environment
    		•
  • Composed of lipid groups with 4 carbon rings attached to each other
  • Different arrangement of atoms and functional groups changes the tpe of steroid
  • Produces hormones within the body, like testosterone and estrogen
    		•
  • Lipids with long carbon chains
  • Solid at room temperature
  • Produced by plants and animals
    		•


    Proteins

  • Made up of amino acid monomers
  • R side chain group determines distinctive shape and function of respective amino acids
  • 20 amino acids = 20 different R groups
  • There are 3 main amino groups
    • Non polar groups for hydrophobic amino acids
    • Polar groups for hydrophilic amino acids
    • Electrically charged group
  • Amino acid polarity determined by R group polarity
  • Amino acids joined by covalent peptide bonds
  • Multiple amino acids are called polypeptides
  • Can be denatured(R group bonds broken) and unfold under certain conditions, like heat



    • Amino Acids

    Primary Structure Secondary Structure Tertiary Structure Quaternary Structure
  • Sequence of a chain of amino acids
  • Linear structure
  • Backbone of polypeptide chain

  • Caused by hydrogen bonding
  • Forms alpha helix or beta pleated sheet
    		•
  • Caused by attractions between alpha helix and beta pleated sheets
  • Caused by different R groups in polypeptide interacting and bonding with each other in aqueous environment
  • Specific structures determined by hydrophobic effect
    • Polar groups directed to outside of structure
    • Non polar groups directed to middle of structure
  • Proteins with multiple polypeptide chains, these polypeptides are arranged into a fourth level of organization

    Nucleic Acids

  • 2 types exist
    • DNA - composed of nucleotides containing sugar deoxyribose
    • RNA - composed of nucleotides containing sugar ribose
  • nucleotides are organic molecules composed of sugar bonded to a phosphate group + a nitrogen containing base
  • DNA has 4 bases: Adenine-thymine, guanine-cytosine
  • RNA has 4 bases: Adenine-uracil, guanine-cytosine
  • Hydrogen bonds between base pairs



    • Enzymes

  • Enzymes serve as catalysts in reactions, lowering the amount of energy needed to chemical reaction
  • Usually names end with -ase (eg. lactase used for lactose)
  • A catalyst is not usd in a reaction
  • Enzymes are composed of long chains of amino acids
  • They form in either primary, secondary, tertiary, and quaternary
  • Enzymes have globular shape with pockets called active sites
  • Active sites are where the catalyst reactions happen
  • Substrates go into active sites and bond with enzyme to allow catalyst to fuction
  • Enzyme inhibitors bind to active site and cause decrease activity in enzyme
