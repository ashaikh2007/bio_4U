Metabolic Processes



Metabolism is the set of life-sustaining chemical reactions in organisms. It includes catabolic (breaking down) and anabolic (building up) processes that convert energy and molecules for cellular functions.


Metabolic Pathways

  • Metabolic pathways are series of chemical reactions occurring within a cell.
  • They are categorized into catabolic (breaking down molecules to release energy) and anabolic (using energy to build complex molecules).
  • Examples include glycolysis, Krebs cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation.


    • Bond Energies: Catabolism and Anabolism

  • Catabolism: Breaks down complex molecules into simpler ones, releasing energy stored in chemical bonds (e.g., glucose → CO₂ + H₂O + ATP).
  • Anabolism: Uses energy to build complex molecules from simpler ones (e.g., amino acids → proteins).


    • Open and Closed Systems

  • Open systems: Exchange both energy and matter with their surroundings (e.g., living organisms).
  • Closed systems: Exchange only energy with their surroundings (e.g., a sealed terrarium).


    • Endergonic and Exergonic Reactions

  • Endergonic reactions: Require energy input (e.g., photosynthesis).
  • Exergonic reactions: Release energy (e.g., cellular respiration).


    • Coupled Reactions

  • Energy released from exergonic reactions drives endergonic reactions.
  • Example: ATP hydrolysis (exergonic) powers muscle contraction (endergonic).


    • Glycolysis

  • Occurs in the cytoplasm.
  • Breaks down glucose (6C) into 2 pyruvate (3C) molecules.
  • Net gain: 2 ATP and 2 NADH.
  • Does not require oxygen (anaerobic).


    • Pyruvate Oxidation

  • Occurs in the mitochondrial matrix.
  • Pyruvate is converted into acetyl-CoA, releasing CO₂ and producing NADH.


    • Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle)

  • Occurs in the mitochondrial matrix.
  • Acetyl-CoA enters the cycle, producing ATP, NADH, FADH₂, and CO₂.
  • Net gain per glucose: 2 ATP, 6 NADH, 2 FADH₂.


    • Electron Transport Chain (ETC)

  • Occurs in the inner mitochondrial membrane.
  • NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons, which pass through protein complexes, creating a proton gradient.
  • Oxygen is the final electron acceptor, forming water.


    • Chemiosmosis

  • Protons flow back into the mitochondrial matrix through ATP synthase, driving ATP production.
  • Net gain: ~28-34 ATP per glucose molecule.


    • Fermentation

  • Occurs in the absence of oxygen (anaerobic).
  • Regenerates NAD⁺ for glycolysis.
  • Types:
    • Lactic acid fermentation: Pyruvate → lactate (e.g., in muscles).
    • Alcoholic fermentation: Pyruvate → ethanol + CO₂ (e.g., in yeast).


    • Photosynthesis

  • Occurs in chloroplasts of plant cells.
  • Converts light energy into chemical energy (glucose).
  • Equation: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂.


    • Structure of a Plant

  • Stomata: Pores on leaves for gas exchange.
  • Thylakoid: Membrane-bound compartments in chloroplasts where light-dependent reactions occur.
  • Grana: Stacks of thylakoids.


    • Light Absorption and Chlorophyll

  • Chlorophyll absorbs light (mainly blue and red wavelengths).
  • Accessory pigments (e.g., carotenoids) capture additional light energy.


    • Light-Dependent Reactions

  • Occur in the thylakoid membrane.
  • Produce ATP and NADPH using light energy.
  • Involve Photosystem II and Photosystem I.
  • Cyclic vs. Non-cyclic photophosphorylation:
    • Non-cyclic: Produces ATP, NADPH, and O₂.
    • Cyclic: Produces only ATP.


    • Light-Independent Reactions (Calvin Cycle)

  • Occur in the stroma of chloroplasts.
  • Use ATP and NADPH to fix CO₂ into glucose.
  • Steps: Carbon fixation, reduction, and regeneration of RuBP.


    • C₄ vs. C₃ Plants

  • C₃ plants: Use the Calvin cycle directly; efficient in moderate conditions.
  • C₄ plants: Adapt to hot, dry climates by separating CO₂ fixation and the Calvin cycle spatially.
