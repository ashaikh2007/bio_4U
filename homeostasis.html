Homeostasis



Homeostasis is the process by which living organisms maintain a stable internal environment despite changes in external conditions. This balance is achieved through complex interactions between the nervous system, endocrine system, and various feedback mechanisms.


Feedback Loops

  • Negative Feedback Loops:
    • Counteract changes to maintain stability.
    • Example: Thermoregulation (body temperature control).
  • Positive Feedback Loops:
    • Amplify changes to complete a process.
    • Example: Oxytocin release during childbirth.


    • Endocrine System

  • Regulates bodily functions through hormones.
  • Exclusive Endocrine Glands:
    • Pituitary gland
    • Thyroid gland
    • Adrenal glands
  • Multifunctional Glands:
    • Pancreas (endocrine and exocrine functions)
    • Gonads (testes and ovaries)
  • Target Cells and Receptor Proteins:
    • Hormones bind to specific receptors on target cells to trigger responses.


    • Steroid vs. Water-Soluble Hormones

    Steroid Hormones Water-Soluble Hormones
  • Lipid-based (derived from cholesterol).
  • Examples: Testosterone, estrogen, cortisol.
  • Diffuse across the lipid bilayer of cell membranes.
  • Bind to intracellular receptors to affect gene expression.

  • Amino acid-based (e.g., peptides, proteins).
  • Examples: Insulin, adrenaline, growth hormone.
  • Bind to receptor proteins on the cell surface.
  • Trigger secondary messengers (e.g., cAMP) to initiate cellular responses.

    Hypothalamus and Pituitary Gland

  • The hypothalamus produces releasing and inhibiting hormones that regulate the pituitary gland.
  • Tropic Hormones: Hormones that stimulate other endocrine glands (e.g., TSH, ACTH).
  • Pituitary Gland:
    • Anterior Pituitary:
      • Produces hormones like growth hormone (GH), thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH), and adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH).
    • Posterior Pituitary:
      • Stores and releases hormones produced by the hypothalamus (e.g., oxytocin, antidiuretic hormone (ADH)).


    • Thyroid Gland

  • Located in the neck, below the larynx.
  • Produces thyroid hormones (T3 and T4) that regulate metabolism.
  • Conditions:
    • Hypothyroidism: Underactive thyroid, causing fatigue and weight gain.
    • Hyperthyroidism: Overactive thyroid, causing weight loss and anxiety.
    • Graves' Disease: Autoimmune disorder causing hyperthyroidism.


    • Adrenal Glands

  • Located on top of the kidneys.
  • Adrenal Medulla:
    • Produces adrenaline and noradrenaline for short-term stress response (fight or flight).
  • Adrenal Cortex:
    • Glucocorticoids (e.g., cortisol): Regulate blood sugar and long-term stress response.
    • Mineralocorticoids (e.g., aldosterone): Regulate electrolyte balance.
    • Gonadocorticoids (e.g., androgens): Influence secondary sexual characteristics.


    • Puberty and Reproductive Hormones

  • The hypothalamus produces gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH), which stimulates the release of FSH and LH from the pituitary.
  • In Females:
    • FSH promotes the development of ovarian follicles.
    • LH triggers ovulation and stimulates estrogen and progesterone production.
  • In Males:
    • FSH stimulates sperm production.
    • LH promotes testosterone production.


    • Menstrual Cycle

  • Follicular Phase:
    • FSH stimulates follicle growth in the ovaries.
    • Estrogen levels rise, thickening the uterine lining.
  • Luteal Phase:
    • LH triggers ovulation (release of an egg).
    • The corpus luteum produces progesterone to maintain the uterine lining.


    • Nervous System

  • Peripheral Nervous System (PNS):
    • Somatic System: Controls voluntary movements.
    • Autonomic System:
      • Sympathetic: Activates fight or flight response.
      • Parasympathetic: Promotes rest and digestion.
  • Neurons:
    • Composed of dendrites (receive signals), cell body, and axon (transmits signals).
    • Glial cells support and protect neurons.
  • Reflex Arc:
    • Sensory neurons detect stimuli and send signals to interneurons in the spinal cord.
    • Motor neurons carry signals to muscles, causing a reflex action.


    • Kidney Function

  • The kidneys filter blood to produce urine, removing waste and regulating electrolytes.
  • Structure:
    • Nephrons are the functional units of the kidney.
    • Blood enters the glomerulus, where filtration occurs.
    • Filtered fluid passes through the proximal tubule, loop of Henle, distal tubule, and collecting duct.
  • Hormones:
    • Antidiuretic hormone (ADH) regulates water reabsorption.
    • Aldosterone regulates sodium and potassium balance.
